## Why the Bear is the basin to prove this on

This is where Rainmaker already operates, and it's the basin my water-balance model is calibrated to (modeled yield ~1.15M AF/yr against ~1.14M observed). It's also the most interesting basin in the country for this exercise, because the Bear doesn't end at a headgate — it's the single largest tributary to the Great Salt Lake, delivering ~1.2M AF/yr. So the value chain runs all the way from a snowflake in the Uintas to a $1.3–2.5 billion lake economy and the air quality of two million people on the Wasatch Front.

That long chain is also where the honesty lives. My model's headline finding stands: the seeding increment that actually reaches the lake is on the order of ~1,200 AF/yr (≈4,700 AF at the basin outlet), which moves the lake's 75-year stage by ~0.02 ft. **Seeding does not refill the Great Salt Lake** — it's 2–3 orders of magnitude short of the structural deficit. The point of this document is not to claim otherwise. It's to show that *every acre-foot has a place in this chain*, that the Bear is a brutal competing-allocation problem, and that water directed to the right use is worth far more than the market currently prices it at.

## Link 3 — First-order use: where Bear water goes

The Bear is over-subscribed and interstate (Wyoming, Idaho, Utah), governed by the Bear River Compact. Roughly four uses compete for every acre-foot:

**Irrigation (the dominant consumer).** The Bear supports 150,000+ acres of irrigated farmland, overwhelmingly alfalfa, pasture, and other hay — over 80% of irrigated land in the Utah portion is forage. My model carries this as a seasonal diversion of ~262K AF, growing-season-shaped, which is what reproduces the river's deep summer low. This is the largest single claim on the water and, per acre-foot, the *lowest-value* use — which is the entire tension of the basin.

**Hydropower.** PacifiCorp runs a run-of-river cascade on the Bear — Soda (14 MW), Grace (33 MW), Oneida (30 MW), plus Cove and the downstream Cutler project in Utah — roughly 77+ MW of installed capacity. Hydropower is *non-consumptive*: the same acre-foot generates at each powerhouse and then continues downstream, so it stacks value without subtracting from other uses.

**Municipal & industrial.** Utah has long eyed the Bear as the next big supply for the Wasatch Front (the proposed Bear River Development), precisely because the growing cities need it. Every AF the cities take is an AF that doesn't reach the lake — the core conflict behind GSL's decline.

**Environmental flow to the Great Salt Lake.** Whatever survives the first three uses reaches the terminal lake. This is the highest-stakes use and the one with no legal champion until recently.

The framework's rule applies hard here: **these are competing allocations of the same water.** A defensible Bear analysis never sums them — it shows the value *by where the acre-foot is directed*, which is exactly the toggle a customer (or the state) wants to explore.

## Link 4 — Second-order value: what each use produces

### Agriculture — the low floor (~$50–150/AF)
150,000+ irrigated acres of alfalfa and grass hay, feeding the region's cattle and dairy. Hay is a low-value-per-acre-foot crop: regional alfalfa needs ~3–4 AF/acre, and the marginal economic value of irrigation water in the Great Salt Lake basin lands in the low tens-to-low-hundreds of dollars per acre-foot. *This is the opportunity-cost baseline for the whole basin* — the price at which water is currently "spent" on its lowest-value use. *Confidence: medium; hay/cattle prices swing, and marginal vs. average value differ.*

### Hydropower — the free stacker (~$4–7/AF, repeated)
A non-consumptive adder. Routing one acre-foot through the Bear cascade's total usable head generates very roughly ~130 kWh, worth ~$4–7/AF at wholesale power prices — and it earns that at each development the water passes. Small per unit, but it's value created *without* taking water from any other use, and it scales linearly with delivered volume. *Confidence: medium; sensitive to my net-head assumption across the cascade — a number I'd refine with PacifiCorp's actual rating curves.*

### The Great Salt Lake economy — the headline, but threshold-shaped
This is where the Bear's value chain gets serious. The lake supports a **$1.3–2.5 billion/yr economy and ~9,000 jobs** through three industries that depend on lake *level and salinity*, not on any single acre-foot:

- **Brine shrimp (Artemia).** A ~$67M/yr cyst harvest supplying ~40% of the world's aquaculture feed. It collapses above a salinity threshold (my scenario model pins ~160 g/L at current volume; the industry fails as the lake shrinks and concentrates).
- **Mineral extraction.** Compass Minerals, US Magnesium and others extract from the brine; falling levels strand intakes and raise costs.
- **Lake recreation & the migratory-bird flyway** (10M+ birds), which underpins tourism and the basin's identity.

The crucial honesty: **lake value is a threshold function of level, not a linear function of marginal inflow.** My model shows full-deployment seeding moves the 75-yr stage ~0.02 ft against a ~5M AF refill deficit. So I will *not* assign a large per-AF lake value to the seeding increment. Instead, the defensible claim is: water *directed* to the lake (whether seeded, conserved, or bought) earns a share of *avoiding* the catastrophic-loss scenario in Link 5 — and the lake is the basin's highest-value destination by orders of magnitude over hay. *Confidence: high on the economy figures, high on the threshold structure, deliberately low on any single per-AF lake number.*

## Link 5 — Third-order value: the community layer

This is the "we give people their lives back" story, made quantitative. Three pieces, in descending order of how confidently I'd present them.

### Lake-effect snow and the ski economy — strongest
The Great Salt Lake generates 5–10% of the Wasatch snowpack and extends the ski season by an estimated 5–7 weeks. Utah's ski industry is ~20,000 jobs and ~$1.2 billion/yr. A shrinking lake weakens the lake-effect engine that helps produce "the Greatest Snow on Earth." This is the cleanest third-order link: Bear water → lake volume → lake-effect snow → a $1.2B industry and the cultural identity of the state. *Confidence: medium-high; the 5–10% snow contribution is established, the dollar attribution to lake level is directional.*

### Avoided economic catastrophe — the framing number
A report for the Great Salt Lake Advisory Council estimates that continued decline could cost **$1.69–2.17 billion/yr and 6,500+ jobs.** This is the avoided-loss anchor: protecting lake level protects this entire downside. It's the number a state audience already takes seriously, and it reframes Bear water from "cheap irrigation input" to "insurance on a multi-billion-dollar economy." *Confidence: high (it's a published commissioned estimate), with the standard caveat that it's a scenario, not a forecast.*

### Dust and public health — real, but I'd present it carefully
As the lake bed exposes, it emits PM2.5; sediment contains arsenic and other metals. One line of evidence projects exposure rising (~24 → 32 µg/m³ in scenarios) and falling disproportionately on communities of color and lower-income residents — an environmental-justice dimension. But the current literature is genuinely mixed: other monitoring finds airborne arsenic still well below health thresholds, and Utah has not yet spent the money it appropriated to quantify the health costs. **My recommendation: present dust-health as a high-importance co-benefit with honest uncertainty, not a hard dollar figure** — and offer to build the quantified version using the bioaccumulation/dust modeling the founder already said he's interested in (an extension of my GSL change-detection and arsenic work). Naming the uncertainty here is what keeps the rest of the chain credible. *Confidence: low on dollars, high on the mechanism and the EJ relevance.*

### Cultural continuity
Underneath all of it: ranching families, ski towns, and a lake the region's identity is built on. Not a line item, but it's the emotional through-line — and the honest version of it is more persuasive than an invented number.

That sentence is the pitch. It's honest about seeding's scale, sharp about the economics, and it positions Rainmaker as the company that can *measure and direct* water value — not just make snow.
